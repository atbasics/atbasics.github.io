Assistive Technology

What is assistive technology?

Assistive technology refers to tools, devices, or systems that support individuals in performing functions that might otherwise be difficult or impossible. These technologies enhance independence, improve quality of life, and promote inclusion for people of all ages and abilities.

History of assistive technology

Assistive Technology has been a part of our society since the earliest known civilizations. One example of assistive technology was the walking cane, which the Ancient Egyptians used not only as a tool but also as a form of status and authority. The walking cane was also used to assist individuals with difficulty walking. Another example is a prosthetic limb from ancient Egypt, estimated to be 2,600-3,400 years old. In 1824, Louis Braille, who had lost his sight as a child, created a system that enabled blind and visually impaired individuals to read and write independently. We now know this system as Braille. Assistive technology has been around for quite some time; we have simply not paid sufficient attention to it.

How Many People Use Assistive Technology?

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), more than 2.5 billion people worldwide use at least one assistive technology product. It is estimated that by 2050, the number of individuals using assistive technology will reach 3.5 billion, driven by an aging population and the prevalence of noncommunicable diseases (long-term health conditions that develop over time and are not contagious; examples include diabetes, cancer, and heart disease).

Types of Assistive Technology

Assistive technologies are commonly categorized as low-tech, mid-tech, or high-tech, based on their complexity and functionality.
Low-tech: Simple, non-electronic tools such as pencil grips, magnifying glasses, or canes.
Mid-tech: Devices that require some power or electronics but are not highly complex, such as audio recorders, talking calculators, or amplified telephones.
High-tech: Advanced electronic or computerized systems, including screen readers, speech-to-text software, and smart home assistants like Alexa.
