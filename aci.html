Aging

The aging and elderly Population

According to the National Council on Aging, 57.8 million adults aged 65 and older were living in the U.S. as of 2022. This number is projected to increase to 78.3 million by 2040. As people age, chronic health issues such as heart disease, arthritis, or diabetes become more common. Many older adults may also experience feelings of loss of independence or of becoming a burden to loved ones as they face challenges with daily activities. Assistive technology plays a crucial role in helping seniors maintain their independence, dignity, and quality of life.

What Happens When You Age?

As people age, they may experience changes such as vision loss, hearing loss, memory challenges, or physical difficulties. It is essential to recognize that aging itself is not a disability. A disability is defined as any condition of the body or mind (impairment) that makes it more difficult for a person to do certain activities. That is not to say that older adults may need to use assistive technologies such as hearing aids, walking canes, or walkers.

Common Age-Related Disabilities

Loss of vision, Age-related macular degeneration, Cataracts and glaucoma, Hearing loss, Dementia, Alzheimers, and Stroke

How Can Assistive Technology Help the Aging and Elderly Population?

Assistive technology (AT) can support older adults by increasing safety, independence, and comfort in daily life. These tools make everyday tasks easier and help seniors stay active, connected, and confident. Some examples of assistive technology that the aging and elderly population may use include:

 - Fall-detection devices
 - Emergency alert buttons

 - Automatic lights and night-light

Chronic Illness

What Is Chronic Illness, and How Many People Have It?

According to the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), chronic illness is defined as “conditions that last 1 year or more and require ongoing medical attention, limit activities of daily living, or both.” While chronic illnesses often do not have a cure, they can be managed through ongoing treatment and support. According to the World Health Organization, over 2.5 billion people worldwide need one or more assistive products, such as hearing aids, wheelchairs, or communication apps. In the United States, more than 90% of adults aged 65 and older have at least one chronic condition. More than 75% of U.S. adults ages 35–64 have at least one chronic illness, and 60% of adults ages 18–34 also report living with a chronic condition.

What are the different health conditions?

Here is a list of just a few health conditions:
 - Diabetes
 - Epilepsy
 - Sickle Cell disease
 - Cancer
 - Dementia (due to any cause)
 - Asthma
 - High blood pressure
 - Heart disease
 - Parkinson’s disease
 - Alzheimer’s disease
 - Endometriosis

How Does Chronic Illness Affect People?

Chronic illness may affect a person's day-to-day life physically, emotionally, and financially. Chronic illness can affect the body by causing fatigue, pain, mobility challenges, changes in organ function, brain fog, and increased stress. It can make daily tasks harder and require ongoing care.

How Can Assistive Technology Help with Chronic Illness?

Assistive technology can make daily life easier, safer, and more manageable for people living with chronic illnesses. It supports independence, reduces physical strain, and helps individuals monitor their health. People may use different types of assistive technology based on their unique needs, and not everyone will use the same tools in the same way. Here are some ways assistive technology may help people manage chronic illness:

Supports Daily Activities

Tools like reachers, grab bars, shower chairs, and automatic can openers make everyday tasks easier and less physically demanding.
Smart home devices (voice controls, smart lights) help conserve energy and reduce physical effort.

Helps Manage Symptoms and Health

Apps for tracking pain, medications, blood pressure, heart rate, or blood sugar
Smartwatches with health monitoring
Reminder systems for medication, appointments, and self-care routines

Reduces Physical Strain

Ergonomic keyboards, adaptive utensils, and joint-friendly tools assist people with chronic pain, arthritis, or fatigue.
Mobility aids such as canes, walkers, and scooters reduce strain on the body and enhance safety.

Improves Communication With Healthcare Providers

Telehealth apps and remote health monitoring tools
Digital logs that track symptoms, activity levels, or medication use
Communication devices for people with speech or breathing challenges

Increases Safety

Emergency alert systems
Fall detection devices
Home sensors that notify caregivers when help is needed

Supports Work and School

Speech-to-text tools for people experiencing pain or fatigue
Larger text options, screen readers, or adjustable displays
Organizational apps that simplify routines and manage cognitive load
